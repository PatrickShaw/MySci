MyScis Aim

MyScis aim is to provide an educational tool for familiarizing students with particular chemistry and physics concepts, this includes:

For Chemistry

       Calculating empirical formulae

       Determining sub-shell configurations

       Exploring the trends in electron negativity and first ionisation energiess.

       Conversions between molarity and mass concentration.

       Calculating the mole of a molecule from the amount of substance in grams.

For Physics

       Conservation of momentum

       Circular motion; acceleration toward the centre of the circular path and velocities at a tangent.

       Relationship between force, mass and acceleration.

       Newtons Universal Law of Gravitation

Controls and Options

Orbit Assist: When creating a particle, MySci will automatically attempts to determine the velocity needed to orbit the closes particle with a mass larger than its own.

Scroll Wheel: Increases and decreases the mass of the next particle created.

G Key: Creates particles with a random mass (relative to the current mass setting) with a random velocity.

Right Click: Tracks the particle you are hovering over. You can stop tracking a particle by pressing the Untrack button

Shift and +: Increases mass

Shift and -: Decreases mass

Save File: Saves the particular instance of the gravity simulator for a later session.

Load File: Loads a previous gravity simulator instance.

How does orbital assist work?

Particles in a circular orbit can use circular motion physics equations, thus:

 

       The magnitude of the force is assumed to be constant

       The mass is constant

       The radius is assumed to be constant

       Thus we can calculate the velocity required for a circular orbit.

       Note that the velocity is relative to the velocity particle being orbited.

Note that the orbital assistance only takes into account the force between the two particles and not any other particles that could alter the orbiting particles path, hence, the orbital assistance does not work well when there are several particles in close proximity to one another.
